Freelance Discovery Call Script

A good discovery call is not a free strategy session. It is a structured qualification conversation that tells you whether the client has a real problem, a real budget, and a project you can safely scope.

Use this script when: prospects arrive with vague requests, you keep writing proposals for bad-fit leads, or discovery calls turn into unpaid consulting.

1. Open the call and set the frame

“Thanks for taking the time. I’ll ask a few questions about the outcome you want, what’s driving the timing, budget range, decision process, and what would make this project successful. If it looks like a fit, I’ll suggest the next step. If not, I’ll point you in a better direction.”

This keeps the call diagnostic. You are not promising ideas, a quote, or a proposal before you know whether the opportunity is real.

2. Confirm the business problem

  • What made this project a priority now?
  • What happens if you do nothing for another 3 months?
  • What outcome would make this worth the investment?
  • Who is affected by the problem today?

3. Qualify budget without sounding awkward

“To make sure I recommend the right level of help, what budget range has already been set aside for this?”
“Projects like this usually fall between [range] depending on scope. Is that aligned with what you had in mind?”

If they cannot share a range, do not skip it. Ask what approval process or constraint is controlling the number.

4. Protect scope before the proposal

  • What deliverables are definitely included?
  • What is explicitly not included?
  • Who will provide copy, assets, approvals, access, or technical inputs?
  • How many decision makers will review the work?
  • What would count as a change request after kickoff?

Scope creep usually starts as missing detail. Capture the detail before you write the proposal.

5. Spot red flags live

Budget mismatch

They want a custom result but only have starter-template budget.

No decision maker

The caller likes the idea but cannot approve money, timeline, or scope.

Vague deadline

“ASAP” with no launch date, dependency, or business reason.

Free strategy fishing

They ask for the full solution before agreeing to a paid next step.

6. Close with one of three next steps

Strong fit: “This sounds aligned. I’ll send a short recap with the scope, assumptions, timeline, and next step by [date].”
Maybe: “I need one more detail before I can recommend a next step: [specific question]. Once I have that, I can tell you whether a proposal makes sense.”
Bad fit: “Based on what you shared, I do not think I’m the right fit for this version of the project. The most useful next step is [alternative].”

7. Send the recap within 24 hours

Subject: Recap and next step for [project]

Thanks for the call today. My notes are: goal: [goal], scope: [scope], constraints: [budget/timeline/approval], risks: [risks]. The best next step is [proposal/paid audit/not a fit]. If this matches your understanding, reply “yes” and I’ll move to the next step.
