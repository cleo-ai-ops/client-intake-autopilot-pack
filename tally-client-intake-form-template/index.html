Fast answer: build your Tally client intake form around six buying signals: service fit, budget, urgency, authority, scope clarity, and readiness. Then score each lead before sending your booking link.

The Tally intake form structure

✓ 1. Identity and contact

Name, email, company/site, role, and the best place to reply. Keep this short so the form does not feel like admin homework.

✓ 2. Project outcome

Ask what they want to improve, launch, fix, or stop struggling with. This gives you the real reason behind the enquiry.

✓ 3. Service fit

Use multiple choice options that match your offers, plus one “not sure yet” option. This prevents vague requests from bypassing qualification.

✓ 4. Budget and deadline

Use ranges instead of an open text field. Ranges are easier for clients to answer and easier for you to score.

✓ 5. Decision process

Ask who signs off, who gives feedback, and whether the budget is already approved. This catches committee risk early.

✓ 6. Scope readiness

Ask about content, access, brand assets, examples, and known constraints. These answers become proposal notes later.

Copy these questions into Tally

  1. What are you trying to achieve with this project?
  2. Why is now the right time to work on it?
  3. Which service do you think you need?
  4. What result would make this project successful?
  5. What budget range is already approved?
  6. What deadline matters, and what happens if it slips?
  7. Who will approve the final decision?
  8. Who else will give feedback during the project?
  9. What assets, content, or access do you already have?
  10. What have you already tried?
  11. What would make this project difficult or risky?
  12. If we are a fit, are you ready to book a discovery call this week?

If your service is high-ticket, keep all 12. If your service is low-ticket, trim to the 7–8 questions that affect whether you can help.

Routing paths after someone submits

9–12 points: send booking link

Reply with a short note referencing their goal, then give one clear call-booking action. Do not make strong leads hunt for next steps.

6–8 points: ask one clarifying question

Do not book yet. Ask the missing question that determines fit: budget approval, deadline reason, decision maker, or scope priority.

0–5 points: decline or downsell

Use a polite decline, a resource, or a paid diagnostic/audit if that fits your business. Protect the calendar.

Scope-risk lead: set boundaries early

If they mention “quick tweaks,” unclear revisions, or many stakeholders, restate how changes and approvals work before proposal time.
