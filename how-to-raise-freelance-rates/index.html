When to raise your rates

Most freelancers wait too long. Here are the signals that you should raise now:

  • You're booking out 3+ weeks ahead. Demand exceeds supply — your price is too low.
  • You haven't raised in 12+ months. Inflation and skill growth compound. A rate that matched your value a year ago doesn't anymore.
  • Every prospect says yes immediately. If nobody pushes back on your price, your price is below market.
  • You've added new skills or certifications. Each new capability increases your delivered value.
  • Your best clients refer you constantly. Referral demand means your reputation exceeds your pricing tier.
  • Comparable freelancers charge 20%+ more. Check Glassdoor, Upwork rate surveys, or industry Slack communities.
Rule of thumb: Raise rates annually at minimum. Top freelancers raise every 6 months for new clients, and annually for existing ones.

The rate increase decision framework

Before sending any rate email, answer these five questions:

  1. What is my current effective hourly rate? Track all hours (including email, revisions, admin) and divide by project revenue. Most freelancers discover their real rate is 30-50% below their quoted rate.
  2. What is the market rate for my skill level? Research 5-10 comparable freelancers. Check their websites, Upwork profiles, or ask in communities.
  3. What value have I delivered recently? List 3 concrete outcomes: "Reduced loading time by 40%", "Delivered 2 weeks early", "Generated $15K in client revenue".
  4. Which clients should I raise, keep, or drop? Not every client gets the same increase. Categorize: A-clients (raise moderately, keep happy), B-clients (raise to market rate), C-clients (raise aggressively or let go).
  5. What is my walk-away rate? The minimum rate at which you'd rather walk than continue. Knowing this makes the conversation easier.

Email template: warm rate increase

Use this for clients you've worked with for 6+ months and have a good relationship with.

Hi [Client Name], I wanted to reach out about an upcoming change to my rates. Working together on [project] has been great — I've really enjoyed [specific thing you enjoy about the work/relationship]. As I continue to invest in [new skill/certification/tool] and expand what I can deliver, I'm updating my rates effective [date, 30+ days out]. My new rate will be [new rate], up from [current rate]. This change reflects: - [Specific value delivered, e.g., "The redesign that increased conversions by 25%"] - [New capability, e.g., "Added analytics and reporting capabilities"] - [Market alignment, e.g., "Alignment with current market rates for this work"] For our current project, the existing rate applies through [end date or project completion]. For any new work after that, the new rate would apply. I'd love to continue working together. Happy to discuss this or answer any questions. Best, [Your name]
Timing: Send 30-60 days before the effective date. Never spring a rate increase on an active project milestone.

Handling the 5 most common objections

1. "That's more than we budgeted"

"I understand budget is a factor. Here are a few options: we can reduce the scope to fit your current budget, keep the full scope at the new rate, or I can recommend a colleague who may be a better fit for your budget. What works best for you?"

2. "Can we negotiate?"

"I've set my rates based on the value I deliver and current market rates. I'm happy to adjust the scope to fit your budget. For example, [scope reduction option]. Would that work?"

3. "Other freelancers charge less"

"That may be the case. My rates reflect [specific experience/certifications/outcomes]. If you share what other freelancers are offering, I can help you compare the value to make the best decision for your project."

4. "We've always paid [current rate]"

"I appreciate that you've been a long-term client. Your loyalty is exactly why I'm giving you advance notice and a more moderate increase than my new-client rate. I'd love to continue working together at the updated rate."

5. "Let us think about it"

"Of course. Take the time you need. I'll follow up on [date, ~1 week]. The new rate applies to any new work starting after [effective date]."

Key principles to remember

  • Rate increases are normal. Every professional service raises rates. You are not doing anything unusual.
  • Losing a client is often a pay raise. If you charge 20% more and lose 1 of 5 clients, you work fewer hours for the same money.
  • Advance notice builds trust. 30-60 days of notice shows professionalism, not greed.
  • Value, not time. Frame the increase around delivered value and outcomes, not "I need more money."
  • Always offer scope options. When a client can't afford the new rate, reduce scope rather than discounting. This preserves your rate integrity.
